This trend involves using Large Language Models (LLMs) to interact with data. Instead of writing complex SQL or Python code, an analyst can ask a question in plain English. The GenAI model then translates this request into a query, executes it against the database, and can even generate a summary or visualization of the results.

B. Real-time Analytics Databases

Traditional data warehouses are great for batch reporting but too slow for live dashboards. Real-time analytics platforms like Apache Druid or ClickHouse are designed to ingest massive streaming datasets and provide sub-second query responses, powering interactive applications that require immediate insights.

C. MLOps for Big Data

MLOps (Machine Learning Operations) applies DevOps principles to the machine learning lifecycle. For Big Data, this means creating robust, automated, and reproducible pipelines for data ingestion, training, evaluation, deployment, and monitoring of ML models at scale. It's about moving from one-off notebook experiments to production-grade, reliable AI systems.

D. Vector Databases

Traditional databases search for exact keyword matches. Vector databases are a new type designed to power semantic search and AI applications. They store data as high-dimensional mathematical representations called "embeddings." When you search, the database finds items that are "semantically similar" or conceptually related, not just textually identical.

Task 2: Mini-Project Final Refinement

Use this time to conduct a final review of your mini-project work so far. Go through the following checklist and make notes of any gaps, areas for improvement, or final questions you have for the instructor.

Project Refinement Checklist:

  • Code & Reproducibility:
    • Is your code clean, well-commented, and organized into a logical notebook or script?
    • If someone else were to run your code, would it work? Have you noted all necessary libraries and setup steps?
    • Are all your data transformations and model parameters justified with comments or documentation?
  • Analysis & Evaluation:
    • Have you evaluated your model using the *right* metrics for your problem (e.g., F1-score for imbalanced data)?
    • Have you interpreted the results? Do you have a section that explains what your model's predictions *mean* in the context of your problem?
    • Have you analyzed feature importances or model coefficients to understand what drives your model's decisions?
  • Report & Presentation Structure:
    • Do you have a clear structure for your final report (e.g., Introduction, Data Understanding, Methodology, Results, Conclusion)?
    • Have you created the key visualizations you will need for your report and final presentation?
    • Is your project's GitHub repository (if applicable) clean and organized?
  • Final Questions:
    • Based on this review, what are the 1-3 most critical questions you need to ask the instructor to ensure your project is a success? Write them down clearly.
